# Hunter S. Thompson: Modes and Periods

Do not write in "Thompson voice in general." The average of his modes is nobody. Pick one register and stay in it.

Each mode has a characteristic length. Match the piece to the length of that mode, not to a generic "Thompson column" length. Overshooting bloats; undershooting produces fragments that read as excerpts rather than pieces.

## Campaign Trail '72 (1972)

The mode of *Fear and Loathing on the Campaign Trail '72*. Reportage at speed. The narrator is physically in motion: hotel rooms, press buses, airports. Deadlines are real, not joked about. Politics are urgent and granular, named, often with unfamiliar detail (a specific aide, a specific pollster, a moment in a hallway). The drug use is present but functional, not ornamental. The voice is young-ish, still chasing the story, angrier than the later work and less resigned.

Use for: pieces set during a specific campaign or event, reportage on something unfolding, pieces where the narrator travels.

Length: long. 2000-5000 words in the book, built from dispatches. A short piece in this mode usually doesn't work.

Avoid: the elegiac late-period tone, the Owl Farm domestic scene, the "I'm too old for this" register.

## Generation of Swine (1985-1988)

The column mode. Weekly pieces of 700-1500 words for the San Francisco Examiner. More structured than the Rolling Stone reportage but looser than the books. Thompson is established, embittered, willing to repeat himself, willing to use the column as a platform rather than a dispatch. The domestic (Owl Farm, Anthony, the neighbours) enters here. Arguments often start local and move outward.

Use for: a column on a current political event in the 700-1500 word range, pieces where the narrator is at home reacting rather than travelling, weekly-columnist rhythm.

Length: 700-1500 words. This is the default for most "write me a Thompson column" requests.

Avoid: the airport-and-hotel furniture of campaign-era work, the extended narrative arcs of the books.

## Hey Rube (2000-2004)

Late ESPN column mode. Nominally sports, actually politics and death and the slow decline of the country. The voice is older, more elegiac, more repetitive (he knows he is repeating himself and lets it stand), funnier in a deadpan way, less controlled. Post-9/11 pieces in this register are particularly angry and unguarded. The register where "we have become a nation of whimpering slaves to Fear" lives.

Use for: pieces reflecting on national mood rather than news, pieces with a sports or gambling texture, late-period political despair, anything set near or after the end of his life.

Length: 600-1200 words, often shorter than Generation of Swine. Post-9/11 pieces run long; earlier pieces are tighter.

Avoid: the tight reportage structure of '72, the mid-period column efficiency.

## Kingdom of Fear register (2003)

The memoir-essay voice from the book of the same name. Reflective, anecdotal, looser in structure, willing to spend paragraphs on a single remembered event. More self-mythologising than the columns, which is either a feature or a bug depending on the reader.

Use for: personal pieces, recollections, work that reaches back decades rather than reacting to now.

Length: variable. Individual essays run 1500-4000 words. Short-form doesn't fit this mode.

Avoid: current-events reportage, any piece where the subject is more important than the narrator's memory of it.

## The Letters (1955-2005, three published volumes)

Not a public register, but essential for understanding the voice. The letters are looser, more obscene, more tender, more uncontrolled than the published work. The Thompson who emerges in the letters is less polished than the Rolling Stone voice, and some of the best-feeling imitations reach for this register rather than the public one.

Use for: pieces framed as letters, fax-style dispatches, late-night-to-a-friend tone, anything where the formal column frame gets in the way.

Length: anything from 200 words to several thousand. The only mode that supports genuine short-form.

## Picking for the present day

If the user asks for Thompson on events after February 2005, the thought experiment places him alive in a period he did not live to see. Pick the mode he would plausibly have written in at that age. Had he lived to 2026 he would be 88 years old; the Hey Rube register carried forward, more tired and more elegiac, is usually the right frame.

Do not write him as a 1972 version of himself commenting on 2026. The young-man voice on old-man subject matter reads as pastiche. The late voice on late subject matter reads as him.
