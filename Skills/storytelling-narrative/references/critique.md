# Narrative Critique & Refinement

Use this reference when the user has existing content (a draft, a deck, a memo, a post) and wants help diagnosing what isn't working or refining it. Also use it as the self-review pass after drafting.

The goal of critique is to find the structural and rhetorical reasons a piece isn't landing - not to layer surface polish on a broken arc. Surface fixes on a piece with no through-line just produce smoother slop.

---

## How to read existing content

Before suggesting any change:

1. **Read it through once at the audience's intended speed.** Note where your attention drifts, where you re-read, where you skip. These are signals.
2. **Find the implicit through-line.** What is the piece actually arguing or telling? Write it as one sentence: "from X to Y because of Z." If you can't, that's the first thing to surface with the user - the piece probably doesn't have one yet.
3. **Find the implicit hook, turn, and resolution.** Where did the loop open? Where did it shift? Where did it close? Often one or more of these is missing.
4. **Note what's working.** What's specific, concrete, alive? These are the load-bearing parts to protect during refinement.
5. **Note what's slop.** Marketing adjectives, generic claims, summary openings, "In conclusion..." closings. Mark them but don't rewrite yet.

Only after this read should you start proposing changes.

## Diagnostic checklist

Run a piece through these questions. Each one points to a different failure mode and a different fix.

### Through-line

- Can you write the through-line in one sentence ("from X to Y because of Z")?
- Does every section, paragraph, or beat serve that sentence?
- Does the through-line drift mid-piece? (Common - the writer started arguing one thing and ended up arguing another.)
- Could the piece be split into two pieces that each argue something cleaner?

**If the through-line is missing or drifting**: this is the first fix. Surface it to the user before touching prose. A piece with a clear through-line but rough prose is salvageable; a piece with great prose and no through-line needs restructuring, not editing.

### Audience

- Is the audience implicitly clear from the piece itself?
- Does the piece meet them where they are (knowledge level, prior beliefs, fatigue)?
- Does it acknowledge what they already know without belabouring it?
- Are there assumptions that don't hold for this audience?

### Hook

- Where does the hook actually land - first sentence, first paragraph, page two?
- Is it specific or generic? (Generic = could open any company's blog post.)
- Does it open a question the reader needs answered?
- Is there setup or throat-clearing in front of it that should be cut?

**Common fix**: cut the first paragraph entirely. The hook is usually paragraph two or three trying to get out.

### Tension and stakes

- Is the tension explicit? (What's wrong, what's at risk, what's about to break.)
- Are stakes named, or only implied?
- Does the piece feel low-stakes throughout? (If everything is "interesting" but nothing is "at risk", the audience won't act.)
- Is there a real antagonist (status quo, broken process, competitor, time pressure) - or is the conflict diffuse?

**Common fix**: name the stakes outright in one sentence near the hook. Implied stakes don't move people.

### Turn

- Is there a turn - a moment where understanding shifts?
- Does it pass the turn test (surprise + shift + actionable "so what")?
- Is it explicitly placed, or buried inside another paragraph?
- Is it concrete and specific, or abstract?
- Is the "so what" stated, or does the audience have to guess?

**The turn is the most often-missing primitive.** Most weak business writing has a status quo, some stakes, and a vague resolution but no clear shift. If the piece feels informational rather than persuasive, missing turn is usually the cause.

**Common fix**: identify what shifted in the writer's understanding while writing this - that's usually the turn that should be foregrounded.

### Resolution

- Is the resolution concrete? (Specific action, decision, new state - not generic outcome.)
- Does it match the through-line?
- Is there a closing summary that adds nothing? ("In conclusion...", "To recap...", "We've explored...")
- For pieces meant to drive action, is the call explicit?

**Common fix**: cut closing summary paragraphs. Land on the resolution itself.

### Pacing and structure

- Is the piece front-loaded with setup that should be cut?
- Are there beats that don't move the through-line and could be cut?
- Is there a section where pace drops - too much detail, repeated point, lost momentum?
- Are paragraphs the right size? (Too long = reader skims; too short = staccato.)
- For format-specific structural rules, see `references/formats.md`.

### Prose

- Concrete nouns over abstract ones?
- Strong verbs over verb+adjective combos?
- Sentence length varied?
- Active voice as default?
- Specific people, numbers, moments - or generic categories?
- Slop tells (see below)?

## Slop tells

These signal AI-generated or marketing-massaged writing. Flag them when you see them.

**Marketing adjectives that mean nothing**: comprehensive, robust, best-in-class, feature-rich, production-ready, enterprise-grade, innovative, transformative, cutting-edge, world-class, next-generation, holistic, multifaceted.

**Filler verbs**: leverage, harness, foster, bolster, underscore, streamline, facilitate, empower, delve into, dive into.

**Empty intensifiers**: seamlessly, pivotal, truly, really, basically, simply, essentially, generally, just.

**AI sentence openers**: "Additionally...", "Furthermore...", "Moreover...", "It's worth noting...", "It's important to note...", "Notably...".

**AI structure tells**: "key insights", "key takeaways", "hot takes", "core principles" used as headers without specifics underneath. Lists of three when the content needed two or four. "In today's fast-paced world..." / "In an ever-changing landscape..." openers. "In conclusion..." / "To summarise..." closings.

**Hedging that drains conviction**: "you might want to consider...", "it could be argued that...", "some would say...", "it's possible that...". Either make the claim or remove it.

**Replacement strategy**: don't substitute one slop word for another. Restructure the sentence around concrete nouns and strong verbs. If the sentence carries no information once the slop is stripped, delete it.

## Common failure patterns

**The list-pretending-to-be-a-story.** Five points connected by "and... and... and...". Fix: find the through-line and cut anything that doesn't serve it. Often this means dropping 2-3 of the points entirely.

**The buried lede.** The most interesting beat is in paragraph six. Fix: move it to paragraph one (or directly after the hook).

**The thesis-statement opening.** Piece opens by announcing what it will argue. Fix: cut the thesis paragraph, drop the reader into the action, let the argument emerge.

**The drift mid-piece.** Starts arguing X, ends arguing Y. Fix: pick one. Keep the more important argument. The other becomes a separate piece (or gets cut).

**The under-built turn.** The shift is named in passing rather than being the climax. Fix: slow down at the turn (the hourglass technique). Add detail. State the old belief explicitly before the new one. Make the "so what" its own sentence.

**The slop drift.** Specific opening, abstract middle, slop closing. Fix: rewrite the abstract middle in concrete terms. Cut the slop closing. The piece often ends up shorter and stronger.

**The horoscope.** Vague enough to apply to anyone. Fix: name names. Add specific numbers, customers, dates, decisions. If the piece could be about a different company, it isn't about this one.

**The features-first pitch.** Leads with what we built rather than what changed for the customer. Fix: invert. Lead with the customer's situation, then the shift, then the product as the cause of the shift.

**The "comprehensive overview".** Tries to cover everything; lands nothing. Fix: pick the single most important thing and serve it. Park the rest.

## Self-review pass for drafted output

Before delivering drafted prose to the user, run this short pass:

- [ ] Through-line still holds across the whole piece
- [ ] Hook is specific and lands within the first paragraph (or first 60 seconds)
- [ ] Turn is explicit and concrete
- [ ] Stakes are named, not only implied
- [ ] Resolution is concrete (specific new state or action)
- [ ] No closing summary paragraph
- [ ] No slop adjectives, filler verbs, AI sentence openers
- [ ] Sentence length varies
- [ ] Concrete nouns / specific examples present
- [ ] Voice matches what the user signalled / sounds human

Fix anything that fails before returning the output. If a structural issue surfaces (turn missing, through-line drifting), surface it to the user rather than silently rewriting - they may have a reason for the choice.
