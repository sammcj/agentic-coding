# Narrative Frameworks (Lenses, Not Templates)

These frameworks are different surface arrangements of the same underlying primitives: **status quo → tension → stakes → turn → resolution**. Treat them as lenses to check material against, not templates to fill in. The right lens for a piece depends on audience, goal, length, and material - not on which framework is "best".

Most strong business narratives use a coarse-grained lens at the document level (e.g. SCQA for an exec memo, Three-Act for a blog post) and a fine-grained lens at the paragraph or beat level (e.g. ABT for a single update or section opener).

## Selection guidance

| Scenario | Primary lens | Useful supporting lens |
|---|---|---|
| Exec update / status memo / Slack post | ABT | SCQA |
| Exec briefing / consulting memo / 1-pager | SCQA | Pyramid Principle |
| Long written report for time-poor execs | Pyramid Principle | SCQA |
| Blog post / article / general talk | Three-Act | ABT (paragraph level) |
| Change story / "how X became Y" | Pixar | Three-Act |
| Vision / mission / manifesto | Golden Circle | Sparkline |
| Persuasive keynote / pitch | Sparkline (Duarte) | Golden Circle, StoryBrand |
| Customer-facing marketing / website | StoryBrand SB7 | Three-Act |
| Founder origin / brand history / personal narrative | Hero's Journey (condensed) | Pixar |
| Conference talk with personal story | Five-Second Moment (Dicks) | Sparkline |
| Data presentation / insight report | Dykes Data Storytelling Arc | Sparkline, Heath SUCCESs |
| Final sticky-message check on any piece | Heath SUCCESs | Cron's brain science |

## Short-form lenses

### ABT (And/But/Therefore) - Randy Olson

The shortest workable narrative shape. Three beats:

- **And** - establish the shared context. ("We've grown the platform 40% YoY AND demand is still climbing.")
- **But** - introduce the tension. ("BUT the underlying database can't sustain another doubling in load.")
- **Therefore** - propose the action. ("THEREFORE we need to commit to the migration in Q3.")

**When to use**: exec updates, project status posts, paragraph-level structuring inside a longer piece, elevator pitches, slide subtitles, Slack posts. Anywhere you need clear thinking in 3-5 sentences.

**Why it works**: forces you to identify the actual tension (the "but") rather than chaining facts with "and... and... and...". The "and" → "but" → "therefore" transition is the smallest unit of narrative.

**Failure mode**: "AND... AND... therefore" - no real tension. If your "but" is weak, the whole thing is informational, not narrative.

### SCQA (Situation / Complication / Question / Answer) - Barbara Minto

The Minto-school standard for executive briefings. Four beats:

- **Situation** - the stable backdrop the reader already accepts.
- **Complication** - what changed or is changing that disturbs the situation.
- **Question** - the question the complication raises in the reader's mind.
- **Answer** - your recommendation or finding.

**When to use**: consulting memos, exec briefings, 1-pagers, decision documents, opening paragraphs of formal reports.

**Why it works**: meets executives where they are - they want to know what's at stake and what you recommend, in that order. SCQA naturally surfaces both. The "question" beat is the connector that prevents the answer feeling unmoored.

**Failure mode**: skipping the complication or making it generic. A vague "the market is changing" complication produces a vague answer.

### Pyramid Principle - Barbara Minto

A structural principle for written documents read by people who skim. Top-down rule:

- Lead with the answer (the single governing thesis).
- Group supporting arguments in 2-4 buckets directly underneath.
- Each bucket has its own supporting facts/evidence.
- Reader can stop at any level and still have the gist.

**When to use**: long written reports, board papers, strategy documents, exec-facing PRDs, consulting deliverables. Anywhere the reader will not read linearly.

**Why it works**: time-poor readers can scan headers and section openers and still leave with the conclusion and reasoning.

**Failure mode**: bottom-up writing. If your document builds to its conclusion, it'll fight every reader who scans. Restructure top-down.

**Note**: Pyramid Principle is structural, not narrative. Combine it with SCQA at the section openings to add narrative tension.

## General-purpose arcs

### Three-Act Structure

The default narrative shape for most longer business writing. Three acts:

- **Act I - Setup**. Context, characters (in business: people, teams, market), the world as it is. Ends with an inciting incident - something that disturbs the status quo.
- **Act II - Confrontation**. Rising tension, obstacles, attempts that don't fully work, the turning point. Most of the running time lives here.
- **Act III - Resolution**. The decision, the action, the new equilibrium. Short - once the turn has happened, end soon.

**When to use**: blog posts, articles, general talks, longer narratives where you have room to breathe.

**Why it works**: matches the audience's pattern-prediction machinery. The brain expects setup-conflict-resolution and stays engaged because each act sets up the next.

**Failure mode**: bloated Act I. Most weak business writing spends too long on setup before the inciting incident. Cut setup ruthlessly - get to the turn.

### Pixar's "Once upon a time..."

Pixar's six-beat structure for stories about change:

- **Once upon a time...** - the ordinary world.
- **Every day...** - the routine, the way things normally are.
- **One day...** - the disruption, the change, the new arrival.
- **Because of that...** - first-order consequence.
- **Because of that...** - second-order consequence (the chain reaction).
- **Until finally...** - the resolution, the new equilibrium.

**When to use**: change stories, adoption stories ("how we moved from X to Y"), case studies, customer journey stories, transformation narratives.

**Why it works**: the doubled "because of that..." forces causal storytelling - each beat must follow from the previous one. This is what separates a story from a list of events.

**Failure mode**: the chain breaks. If your second "because of that" doesn't actually follow from the first, you don't have a story - you have two adjacent things.

### Hero's Journey (condensed) - Joseph Campbell

A condensed business-friendly version of Campbell's monomyth, useful for founder, brand-origin, and personal narratives:

- **Call to adventure** - the catalyst, the moment of recognition.
- **Crossing the threshold** - the commitment (quitting the job, taking the leap, raising the round).
- **Trials, allies, enemies** - the challenges, mentors, competitors along the way.
- **Ordeal** - the near-failure, the dark moment.
- **Reward** - the breakthrough, the win, the lesson.
- **Return with the elixir** - coming back changed, with something to share.

**When to use**: founder stories, company origin narratives, personal brand pieces, "lessons learned" pieces, conference keynotes that are partly autobiographical.

**Why it works**: structurally familiar to any audience that's seen a film in the last fifty years. The vulnerability of the ordeal is what builds trust.

**Failure mode**: skipping the ordeal. Founder stories without a real near-failure read as humblebrag. The ordeal is what makes the journey relatable.

## Persuasion & vision lenses

### Golden Circle (Why / How / What) - Simon Sinek

Three concentric rings, communicated outward-in:

- **Why** - the belief, the purpose, the reason the work exists. ("We believe...")
- **How** - the unique approach or principle that follows from the why.
- **What** - the actual product, service, or output.

**When to use**: vision and mission communication, manifestos, all-hands talks, investor pitches that need to land purpose before product, brand-level pieces.

**Why it works**: leads with belief, which the audience either shares or doesn't - and that filtering creates resonance with the right people. Product features are forgettable; reasons to believe stick.

**Failure mode**: a vague or unfalsifiable why. "We believe in helping people" is too generic. The why must be specific enough that someone could reasonably disagree.

### Sparkline - Nancy Duarte (from "Resonate")

A persuasive presentation oscillates between current state and possible future:

```
What Is → What Could Be → What Is → What Could Be → ... → New Bliss
```

Three contrasts to use:
- **Content contrast** - your view vs. the audience's prior view.
- **Emotion contrast** - analytical content followed by emotional content.
- **Delivery contrast** - traditional vs. nontraditional delivery moments.

End with **new bliss** - a vivid picture of the world if the idea is adopted, plus a clear call to action.

**When to use**: persuasive keynotes, change-management talks, big-vision pitch decks, town halls that need to move people to action.

**Why it works**: the gap between "what is" and "what could be" is where the audience's energy lives. Each oscillation widens the gap and pulls them toward the future.

**Key supporting concepts**:
- **The Big Idea** - one sentence: situation + complication leading to your unique recommendation.
- **S.T.A.R. moment** - Something They'll Always Remember: a dramatic, tangible demonstration of the idea (Jobs pulling MacBook Air out of an envelope).
- **Audience as hero** - the audience is the protagonist; the speaker is the mentor.
- **Sound bites** - small, repeatable phrases that travel.

**Failure mode**: only one oscillation, or weak "what could be". The future state has to be vivid and specific.

### StoryBrand SB7 - Donald Miller

Seven-element framework for customer-facing brand messaging. The customer is the hero; the brand is the guide.

1. **A character (the customer)** - wants something specific.
2. **Has a problem** - at three levels:
   - **External** - the tangible problem.
   - **Internal** - the emotional frustration. (*Customers buy solutions to internal problems.*)
   - **Philosophical** - why it's wrong that this problem exists.
3. **And meets a guide (the brand)** - who shows **empathy** ("I understand") + **authority** ("I've solved this before").
4. **Who has a plan** - clear steps (3-4 max), reducing perceived risk.
5. **And calls them to action** - direct ("Buy now") and transitional ("Download the guide").
6. **That helps them avoid failure** - explicit negative stakes.
7. **And ends in success** - the after-state, the transformation.

**When to use**: website copy, marketing pages, sales decks, product positioning, brand messaging, customer-facing pitches.

**Why it works**: positions the brand correctly (guide, not hero) which is counter to most companies' instinct. Names the internal problem, which is what actually drives buying decisions.

**Failure mode**: positioning the brand as the hero. The audience is not interested in your journey; they're interested in theirs.

## Data & insight lenses

### Data Storytelling Arc - Brent Dykes

Based on Freytag's Pyramid, optimised for fact-based stories. Four beats:

- **Setting & hook** - just enough context, then a notable observation that opens a question (the hook).
- **Rising points** - focused supporting evidence that builds tension. Not a data dump.
- **Aha moment** - the central insight: an unexpected shift in understanding plus an explicit "so what". This is the climax. Don't put it upfront.
- **Solution & next steps** - options and a recommendation.

Dykes' core formula: **Data + Narrative + Visuals** - all three required. Eye-appealing charts without strong narrative are weak data stories. Reporting is not storytelling.

**The insight test** (an aha moment must do all three):
- **Surprise** - does it challenge what we believed?
- **Shift understanding** - does it change how the audience thinks about the problem?
- **Inspire action** - does it create urgency to do something differently?

**When to use**: data-driven presentations, insight reports, stakeholder briefings about findings.

**Failure mode**: putting the insight first ("executive summary" instinct). That kills the suspense the rising points are meant to build.

## Performance storytelling

### Five-Second Moment - Matthew Dicks

A performance-storytelling framework from a 61-time Moth StorySLAM champion. Useful in business when you need to drop in a personal anecdote.

**Core concept**: every story is about a single moment of transformation that takes no more than five seconds. Without it, you have an anecdote, not a story.

**Construction rules**:
- **Start at the end** - know your moment first, build backward.
- **Begin as close to the end as possible** - strip setup that doesn't serve the moment.
- **Begin in the opposite** - open in the emotional or situational opposite of where you'll land.
- **The Dinner Test** - if you wouldn't tell it this way to a friend at dinner, don't tell it that way at all.
- **Stories can never be about two things** - one five-second moment per story.
- **Use "but" and "therefore"; never "and"** - "and" provides no momentum.

**Engagement devices**: stakes, surprise, suspense, backpacks (info loaded in advance), hourglasses (slowing down right before the moment), breadcrumbs, crystal balls (false predictions).

**When to use**: opening anecdotes in talks, vulnerability moments in keynotes, conference talks, leadership comms with a personal beat.

**Failure mode**: a story about two things. If you can't say what the single moment of change is, the story isn't ready.

## Diagnostic / cognitive lenses

### Brain Science of Story - Lisa Cron ("Wired for Story")

Why stories work neurologically. Use as a diagnostic when something isn't landing.

| Cognitive truth | Story implication |
|---|---|
| Brain evaluates everything via "will this help me?" | Answer "what's in it for me?" from the first beat. |
| Emotion drives meaning. | If the audience isn't feeling, they aren't paying attention. |
| Action is goal-directed. | A protagonist (or narrative) without a clear goal has nothing to figure out. |
| We see the world as we believe it to be. | Show when and why existing understanding broke (= the turn). |
| We think in concrete images, not abstractions. | Anything conceptual must be made tangible in specific examples. |
| Brain craves cause and effect. | Every beat must causally connect to the next; randomness is rejected. |
| Brain seeks patterns. | Set patterns up; break them at the turn. |

**When to use**: diagnostic - when a piece isn't landing, walk through these and find the missing primitive.

### SUCCESs - Chip & Dan Heath ("Made to Stick")

A diagnostic checklist for sticky messages. Run a piece through each letter:

| Principle | What it means | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple** | Single core message | Can the audience repeat it after one reading? |
| **Unexpected** | Breaks an expected pattern | Does it open a knowledge gap? |
| **Concrete** | Tangible, specific | Could a stranger picture it without you explaining? |
| **Credible** | Anchored in verifiable detail | Are claims tied to evidence? |
| **Emotional** | Connects to people, not categories | Does it activate self-interest or identity? |
| **Stories** | Embedded narrative | Is there a real arc, not just facts? |

**The Curse of Knowledge**: once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it. This is the single biggest barrier to sticky communication. Combat it with concrete language and the Dinner Test.

**When to use**: final review pass on any piece. If a letter scores weak, the piece needs work there.
