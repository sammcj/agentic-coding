# Hooks & Turns: The Engine of Narrative

Hooks open attention loops. Turns close them. Together they power every compelling narrative - pitch, post, talk, memo, or report. Most weak business writing fails at one of these two points: the hook is generic and buried, or the turn is missing.

---

## What makes a hook work

The brain is a prediction machine. A hook works by creating a **knowledge gap** - a space between what the audience knows and what they want to know. This gap triggers dopamine release (Lisa Cron), which says "pay attention, this matters."

A working hook does two things:
1. Signals that something unexpected, important, or threatening is happening
2. Creates an open question the audience needs answered

**Why hooks fail**:
- **No stakes.** "Here's our Q3 update" - who cares?
- **No gap.** The answer is obvious or already known.
- **Too abstract.** The brain needs specifics to engage. We think in images, not abstractions.
- **Buried.** The setup goes on too long before the hook lands.
- **Generic.** A hook that could open any company's blog post about anything is not a hook.

**Hook placement**:
- Live presentation: within the first 30-60 seconds.
- Blog post / article: in the first paragraph, ideally the first sentence.
- Pitch / memo: before the recipient decides whether to keep reading. For exec audiences, often the very first line.

## Six hook types

### 1. The anomaly hook

Present a data point, observation, or situation that breaks an expected pattern. The audience's brain registers "that doesn't fit" and demands resolution.

**Pattern**: "Everything looked normal - until [unexpected observation]."

Examples:
- "Revenue grew 12% last quarter across every region - except Southeast, which dropped 23%."
- "We've shipped on schedule for eight straight quarters. Then in March, three releases slipped at once."
- "Most of our churn happens in month one. The customers who churned this quarter had been with us for over two years."

**Why it works**: the brain hates unexplained pattern breaks. It wants resolution.

### 2. The stakes hook

Open with what's at risk: financial, operational, reputational, human, or strategic.

**Pattern**: "If we don't [act], [consequence]."

Examples:
- "At current churn rates, we'll lose $4.2M in recurring revenue by Q4."
- "We have nine months before our biggest competitor ships the equivalent feature."
- "Every week we delay this migration, the rollback window gets harder to recover."

**Why it works**: the brain prioritises survival-relevant information. Stakes activate attention systems faster than information does.

### 3. The contrast hook (Duarte's "what is / what could be")

Juxtapose the current state against a possible future to create tension between them.

**Pattern**: "Right now [what is]. But imagine [what could be]."

Examples:
- "Right now, our reviewers spend six hours per document. With the right system, that could be 45 minutes."
- "Today, our customers wait three weeks for an answer. We can get that to three minutes."
- "Today every team writes its own auth middleware. By December, no team should have to."

**Why it works**: contrast creates cognitive dissonance. The gap between "what is" and "what could be" generates desire for resolution.

### 4. The question hook

Pose a question the audience can't help but try to answer.

**Pattern**: "What if...?" or "Why does...?" or "How is it that...?"

Examples:
- "Why do 40% of our highest-value customers leave within 90 days of onboarding?"
- "What would change if we could deploy to production in five minutes instead of fifty?"
- "How is it that our smallest customers generate the most support load?"

**Why it works**: the brain is goal-directed. A question gives it a goal. It cannot *not* try to answer.

### 5. The confession / vulnerability hook

Start with a personal moment of failure, surprise, or realisation. Most business writing avoids this; that's why it works when used.

**Pattern**: "I was wrong about..." or "I didn't expect..." or "We thought we knew X. We didn't."

Examples:
- "When I first saw the support data, I assumed the new product was failing. I was wrong."
- "Three months ago, I would have told you we were on track. The numbers said otherwise."
- "We launched what we thought was the obvious feature. Nobody used it."

**Why it works**: vulnerability builds trust instantly. Audiences lean in because authenticity is rare. The brain attends to characters who have something at stake - including the speaker.

### 6. The "in medias res" hook

Start in the middle of the action. Skip all preamble. Drop the audience into a specific moment and let the setup come later.

**Pattern**: a concrete, specific moment with sensory or situational detail.

Examples:
- "The alert fired at 2:47am. The system was about to breach its safety threshold."
- "Slide 47 - that's where the presentation fell apart in front of the executive team."
- "The customer's email was three lines long. The third line was 'we're switching to your competitor on Monday'."

**Why it works**: specificity creates mental imagery. Action creates momentum. The audience is instantly inside the scene rather than being walked into it.

## What makes a turn work

The **turn** is the moment, finding, decision, or argument that shifts the audience from their old understanding to a new one. It is the load-bearing primitive of narrative - and the one most often missing or under-built in business writing.

A working turn combines:
1. **A shift in understanding** - something the audience now sees differently.
2. **An explicit "so what"** - why the shift matters.

A finding without a "so what" is an observation, not a turn. An interesting fact is not a turn unless it changes how the audience should act, decide, or believe.

### The turn test

For any candidate turn, ask three questions:

- **Surprise?** Does it challenge what the audience believed coming in? (If a dog bites a mailman, that's not a story. If a mailman bites a dog, it is.)
- **Shift understanding?** Does it change how the audience thinks about the problem, not just add a new fact?
- **Inspire action?** Does it create a reason to do something - decide, choose, change?

If it doesn't pass all three, it's a beat, not a turn.

### Placement

**The turn usually lands mid-to-late, not upfront.** The "executive summary" instinct that puts conclusions first kills the narrative tension that makes the turn land. Setup → tension → turn → resolution is the shape that works.

Exception: written documents read by people who scan (board papers, exec memos, consulting reports) often need bottom-line-up-front. In that case, give a one-sentence headline up top, then tell the story underneath. The reader who scans gets the answer; the reader who keeps reading gets the narrative.

### Making the turn land

Techniques drawn from across frameworks:

- **The hourglass** (Dicks) - slow down right before the turn. Add detail, a beat, a pause. This signals "this is important" and lets the audience catch up emotionally.
- **Contrast framing** (Duarte) - state the old belief explicitly before revealing the new understanding. "We assumed X. The data shows Y." The contrast amplifies the shift.
- **Concrete specificity** (Heath) - the turn must be tangible, not abstract. Not "customer behaviour is changing" but "customers who don't get a response within 48 hours churn three times more often within 90 days."
- **Emotional connection** (Cron) - connect the turn to people, not categories. Real customers, real teams, real names where possible.
- **The "so what" bridge** (Dykes) - immediately after the turn, name why it matters. Don't make the audience guess.

## The transformation arc

Every story, at every scale, follows the same shape:

```
Old understanding → tension / evidence → new understanding → new action
```

Different frameworks dress it differently:

| Framework | Shape |
|---|---|
| Three-Act | Setup → Conflict → Resolution |
| Pixar | Once upon a time / Every day → One day → Because of that ×2 → Until finally |
| ABT | And → But → Therefore |
| SCQA | Situation → Complication → Question → Answer |
| Sparkline (Duarte) | What Is → What Could Be (alternating) → New Bliss |
| Dykes Arc | Setting & Hook → Rising Points → Aha Moment → Solution |
| StoryBrand SB7 | Hero wants → Has problem → Meets guide → Gets plan → Acts → Avoids failure → Achieves success |
| Hero's Journey | Call → Threshold → Trials → Ordeal → Reward → Return |

They are all the same shape. The hook opens the loop. The turn closes it. Everything else is the bridge between.

## Quick checklists

### Hook check

- [ ] Lands within the first 30-60 seconds (or first paragraph for written work)
- [ ] Creates a specific, unanswered question
- [ ] Concrete, not abstract
- [ ] Stakes are signalled (not necessarily stated outright)
- [ ] Would pass the Dinner Test - would you open with this telling a friend?
- [ ] Could not appear in a generic blog post about a different company

### Turn check

- [ ] Genuinely surprising - challenges assumptions the audience came in with
- [ ] Shifts understanding, not just adds a fact
- [ ] The "so what" is explicit, not implied
- [ ] Placed at the climax, not the beginning (or paired with a one-line headline if BLUF is required)
- [ ] Concrete and specific, not abstract
- [ ] Connects to real people the audience cares about
- [ ] Naturally leads to a clear action or recommendation
